The majority of Alabama is covered with loblolly pine and oak forests. Loblolly pines aren't unique to Alabama, but they are only found in a handful of southeastern states. The best identifiers for Alabama are the yellow/green/blue license plates and the black and yellow stripes under arrow configuration of certain warning signs. Most state posted signs have either white square stickers or no stickers.

Most urban coverage is in the Phoenix metro region, Tucson, or Yuma. Several small towns in the north have limited coverage, but otherwise, Arizona only has coverage on state roads, US highways, and interstate freeways. The primary Arizona license plate is easiest to identify if you look for the splotch of purple in the bottom left corner. Otherwise, it's often easier to deduce that it's not one of the more easily identifiable New Mexico or Nevada license plates.

Otherwise, the square and rectangle shaped bollards can help you deduce that it's not California or Nevada, which both own their own unique bollards, or New Mexico which uses circle bollards. A very small portion of southern Arizona contains saguaro cacti and distance markers in kilometers. Both are Arizona-specific, but they are also very localized and don't come up very often.

Identification
Colorado can be split into roughly three areas: dry grasslands east of Denver, the Rocky Mountains, and the western side of the Rockies. The standard Colorado license plate is mostly white except for a strip of black at the top that is easily mistaken for a shadow when blurred. However, you can use a the license plates to distinguish from New Mexico, which uses black, yellow, and green plates.

Outside of cities, bollards are the best way to distinguish Colorado from surrounding states. Bollards in Colorado tend to be green or blue, often with a green post. Colorado also has a unique bridge marker and you will occasionally see yellow posts used for signs, which is unique to Colorado. Though the bridge marker might already give it away, Colorado has a habit of building new modern bridges adjacent to older ones, preserving the original bridge structure though it is no longer in use.

Eastern Colorado
The eastern half of the state is mostly dry grasslands. It can look very similar to other midwestern states, especially Kansas and Nebraska. Two ways to determine Colorado over its neighbors is by its green post bollards and by the grid of county roads named with letters instead of numbers. Kansas doesn't use bollards at all, and none of Colorado's neighbors use letters for county roads (though Missouri does)

Small towns consist of one or two-story brick or stone buildings with grid-based concrete streets. The main streets of these towns are usually marked US or State highways.

Northwestern Colorado
North and northwest Colorado are scrub deserts, similar to Utah and Wyoming. US highways are marked by white circle bollards with brown-ish posts. Wyoming also uses white circle bollards with brown posts in similar landscapes, but if you see a bollard with two yellow circles, you're in Wyoming, not Colorado.

Western Colorado
Western Colorado goes from rolling hills to steep gradients depending on what section of the Rockies you're in. Yellow foliage can help distinguish Colorado mountains from Wyoming, Montana, and Idaho. However, the quaking aspens at high altitudes can look similar to birches at more northern latitudes.

Bollards start to double stack into the Rocky Mountains and mile markers are mounted higher.

Denver
Most of Colorado's urban coverage is in the Denver metro area. Near the city's center, the street name and address are separated by a white line. Outside the downtown area, street signs have a symbol unique to that section of the city. See the City Symbols page for a list of Denver-specific street sign meta.

Denver has a relatively extensive public transit network for the United States. Buses are generally white with a blue, orange, and red stripe, although they are often covered with advertisements. Bus stops are red and say "The Ride" on one side and "STOP" on the other.

Denver also has about 30 labeled bike routes between Lakewood and Aurora if you want to get even more specific.

Washington DC is a small diamond-shaped area between Maryland and Virginia. Most of the city has a grid layout with lettered streets running east/west and numbered streets running north/south. Streets named after US states are major diagonals.

The street sign layout is unique, with a cardinal direction on the main sign with a smaller green numbered box below. The cardinal direction is relative to the center of the city, and will tell you which area of the city you're in. Near the city center, road signs will also have red stars and stripes flag. DC is also divided into wards or zones, and you can usually tell which one you're in by looking at nearby parking advisory signs.

About half of Delaware is wheat and corn fields, and the other half is a combination of midwestern and coastal New England vibes. The best meta to look for is the black and gold license plates and the state-wide area code, 302. You may also stumble across Delaware's state symbol, a man riding a horse. Delaware also has a set of state-specific rules that might otherwise appear normal if you weren't looking for them, like the unique fire station sign and adding the text "except left turns" to normal DOT signs.

Identification
Outside of the cities, Mississippi coverage runs along US highway, state, and county roads through loblolly pine and broadleaf forests. The best ways to identify Mississippi from other southeastern states is either the gold-white-gold pattern of license plates or by the road line configuration on newer paved roads.

AL and MS road line comparison

All bridges with concrete barriers, including interstate overpasses, will have a green marker with a white number unique to Mississippi. To distinguish Mississippi from Alabama at a signed T-intersection, Alabama always includes a yellow and black stripe pattern, while Mississippi just displays the arrow. Mississippi also uses black writing on the back side of state-issued signs instead of stickers, but so does Louisiana and Arkansas, and sometimes the writing has faded in the sun to the point that it can't be seen in blurry coverage.

In rural areas, Mississippi can be distinguished from other southeastern states by the middle road line pattern. Mississippi has three separate tracks, two for solid lines and one for dashed lines. Other states use one or two tracks, centering the lines in the middle. It is most apparent in the double yellow line configuration which appears wider in Mississippi than in other states. The single dashed line configuration is the same, but the single solid and single dashed line configuration will appear shifted to the solid side in Mississippi, but will be centered in other states. In newly paved roads, the split in the asphalt runs through the dashed line in Mississippi and runs between the dashed and solid lines in other states.

Nebraska's geography looks similar to the other midwestern plains states, especially Kansas. Nebraska's interstate highway system is concrete in and around the major cities, but asphalt across the plains. Nevada is a bit unique because identifying you're in this state is easy, but identifying *where* in the state is difficult. The state has unique white mile markers and distinctive bollards with black plating and four bolts, one on each corner. Each mile marker says the road you're on, then a two letter designation for the county, and the distance from the road's source. A list of county abbreviations can be found here. You'll occasionally find temporary plastic bollards, but Nevada is otherwise among the most consistent states with bollards.

East of Oklahoma City, the state is fairly green with plentiful broadleaf trees. West of Oklahoma City and into the panhandle, there are fewer trees and it looks more like the typical midwest grasslands. In all parts of the state, the dirt looks red when exposed. In 2016, Oklahoma changed its standard license, so you may find one of two license plates, depending on coverage year. Many intersections will feature green signs, unique to Oklahoma, with just two or three white numbers. Smaller signs with only two numbers will appear in older coverage, but newer coverage will feature larger signs with three numbers. The top number will be anywhere from 1 to 77, referencing the county this intersection is in. The list is in alphabetical order, starting at one, Adair County, all the way to seventy-seven, Woodward County. The second number is a reference number for state maintenance that you can read more about here. The third optional number is the distance into that particular section this sign currently is.

State highway bollards will vary by county, but drainage tunnels and grates are marked with three yellow reflectors or a similar yellow substitute. Most signs have cross-braces behind them, which is most apparent when two or more signs are arranged horizontally on a single post. Oregon has roughly three different geographic regions, separated by the Cascade Range. East of Bend and Klamath Falls, Oregon looks similar to the scrub deserts of Nevada and Wyoming. The south side of the Cascades has dry pine forests, while the north side has the stereotypical ferns and trees with mossy branches of the Pacific Northwest. On the west side of the Cascades, the pine forests dry out and start to include oaks and other broadleafs closer to the Pacific Coast.
