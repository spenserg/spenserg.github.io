Identification
The majority of Alabama is covered with loblolly pine and oak forests. Loblolly pines aren't unique to Alabama, but they are only found in a handful of southeastern states. The best identifiers for Alabama are the yellow/green/blue license plates and the black and yellow stripes under arrow configuration of certain warning signs. Most state posted signs have either white square stickers or no stickers.

Interstates
Alabama is not consistent with bollard usage on interstate highways. A better way to distinguish Interstates in Alabama is a green sign with a mile marker and name of the crossing road on each overpass. Among the states that have loblolly pines, Alabama is the only state that posts these signs.

Overpass sign

Interstate offramps use a small pointed arrow, which can distinguish it from Mississippi that doesn't use offramp arrows and Georgia, whose offramp arrows are made up of two separate shapes that have flat ends.

Identification
The majority of Alaska coverage is an asphalt single lane state highway running through spruce and birch forests. Some coverage is low quality or fogged over, and you can often spot another google car either leading or following. Guardrails will usually have tall yellow bollards, but you'll sometimes see temporary white ones or none at all. Mountains with glaciers against a yellow and white birch forest can look similar to the quaking aspens at high altitudes in Colorado.

Identification
Most urban coverage is in the Phoenix metro region, Tucson, or Yuma. Several small towns in the north have limited coverage, but otherwise, Arizona only has coverage on state roads, US highways, and interstate freeways. The primary Arizona license plate is easiest to identify if you look for the splotch of purple in the bottom left corner. Otherwise, it's often easier to deduce that it's not one of the more easily identifiable New Mexico or Nevada license plates.

Typical Arizona scrub desert

Plate with purple in lower left corner

Outside of metro areas, the black grid warning sign is the best clue if you are near an intersection. Otherwise, the square and rectangle shaped bollards can help you deduce that it's not California or Nevada, which both own their own unique bollards, or New Mexico which uses circle bollards. A very small portion of southern Arizona contains saguaro cacti and distance markers in kilometers. Both are Arizona-specific, but they are also very localized and don't come up very often.

Interstates
In general, Arizona interstates are easier to identify than US highways or state highways. Interstate overpasses list the clearance height and crossing road name on green signs, with the exception of some in downtown Phoenix that do not. Offramps and onramps will always have a labeled green marker, and will use either metal square bollards or the plastic rectangle ones.
